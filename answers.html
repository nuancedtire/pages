Tell me about yourself

I’m an emergency medicine clinician at Barts Health, currently working at Whipps Cross, and in parallel I’ve spent the last several years building clinical AI and healthcare software. Clinically, I work across resus, majors and ambulatory care, so I’m very close to the documentation burden, pathway complexity and real-world workflow friction clinicians face every day. Technically, I’ve built and shipped several healthcare AI products — most recently Aide, a RAG-based clinical guideline retrieval platform for ED staff, with source-cited answers, feedback capture, analytics and audit trails. I’ve also worked as a founding engineer on Peerr.io and Aiigent.io, building LLM-powered clinical workflow features, and earlier worked at DailyRounds/Marrow, helping scale a medical education product to over 500,000 users. What attracted me to Heidi is that it sits exactly where I want to work: clinically grounded AI, shipped into real workflows, with safety, usability and adoption treated as first-class engineering problems.

Why Heidi?

Heidi feels unusually aligned with my background. In emergency medicine I see daily how admin and documentation pull clinicians away from patients. What stands out is that Heidi isn’t just a demo or research project — it’s deployed into real clinical workflows and has to earn trust from clinicians in messy, high-pressure environments. I’ve already been building in a similar direction with Aide, trying to make clinical information retrieval safer, faster and more usable in ED settings. Heidi is operating at a scale where these ideas can genuinely change day-to-day clinical work.

Why move from medicine into engineering?

I’m not moving away from medicine because I dislike clinical work — my clinical experience is the reason I care about this space. But the problems I keep wanting to solve are system and workflow problems: documentation burden, fragmented guidelines, poor tooling, unsafe handovers, and clinicians having to work around software rather than with it. As a clinician I can help the patients in front of me. As a clinical engineer, I can build tools that help thousands of clinicians deliver better care.

Tell me about Aide

Aide is an AI-assisted clinical guideline retrieval platform I built for emergency departments. It lets ED staff ask plain-English questions about local guidelines, pathways, contact numbers and equipment locations, and get source-cited answers. I deliberately designed it not to diagnose, triage or recommend treatment — it retrieves and presents approved information with citations. The main learning was that in clinical AI, the hard problem is not just model performance. It’s trust, governance, updateability, user behaviour and designing around uncertainty.
